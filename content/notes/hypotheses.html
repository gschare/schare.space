Hypotheses on convivial software

29 April 2026

And a few other things I happened to think about.

  1. Beneath the movements for software to be open, customizable, programmable, democratic, extensible, and malleable is the more honest desire for software to be delightful. Malleability is a property of systems. Delightfulness is a property of human experiences.

  2. No sooner will human-computer interaction become aware of its structuralist ideology than it will turn towards poststructuralism.

  3. Software engineering in the age of LLMs has only become more interesting and more rich as a treasure chest of anthropological insights derived from expert knowledges. If anything, analysis of programmers’ conceptual “moves” free from the cruft of real code will reveal the pure essence of software design. From there will emerge and develop a true science of software design.

  4. Concept design must contend with other design frameworks, like value-sensitive design, by articulating precisely how it answers the meta-questions of the field of design in the context of software and hardware.

  5. Engineering students will be taught to think not just about the pipe and how it is designed and built but also the oil that flows through it and where the oil comes from. Social science students will be taught to think not just about the oil and where it comes from but also the pipe that carries it and how the pipe is designed and built.

  6. Software engineering and programming happen differently in different communities. The shadows of the differences in these cultural practices reveal the essential underlying aspects of programming.

  7. The novel is dead. But, despite the exciting potential for short-form text content and interactive media and microblogging, computational writing is not yet healthy enough to take its place. Instead we sit in this awkward middle place of short video without a real culture.

  8. By a sober accounting of political, social, and economic trend it appears overwhelmingly likely that this generation will not flourish, will not be a golden age. Indeed it looks at risk of a dark age, where all the knowledge and social progress of the 20th century will be lost. In that event there is no cause for alarm; instead we must embrace the responsibility of stewarding cultural and intellectual traditions until the next renaissance.

  9. The internet is a psychedelic substance that has not been treated as such.

  10. The internet is for hanging out with my friends.

  11. The internet can be for civic life; it can realize the concept of a society.

  12. An unfortunately overlooked and undersupported form of asynchronous online communication is commentary. It is good to recapitulate. In fact it is the basic essence of teaching.

  13. Programming is probably a very unique thing, cognitively speaking. The ancient Greeks were interested in how the technology of writing would affect the way we think; should we not approach programming with the same curiosity?

  14. AI-assisted writing (and other creative activities) will not flourish until designers of augmented writing tools embrace the fact that creativity is the process of bridging consciousness and unconsciousness and the job of the writer is to say in words what cannot be said in words.

  15. The improvement of software and the turning of its ends toward the good of the society subjected to it hinges on the development of a language of discontent—a critique of the form—which can state, precisely and imaginatively, the successes and the shortcomings in terms that software designers themselves care about and which can lead proactively to creative redesign and counterprogramming.

  16. Software that can be shared is not inherently better than software than cannot, but it would benefit public health if we had good and conceptually simple ways by which people could write programs and share them with other people. Static JavaScript accessed by universal resource locator is a miracle that deserves more praise but it does not go far enough—the people require persistent shared context!

  17. Deep linking is a notion that deserves much more exploration.

  18. The design of extensible languages and systems will be a central part of software design curricula. The theory of extensibility is incomplete. It probably has something to do with the philosophy of interpretation.

  19. A population denied convivial cities struggles to imagine convivial internets.

  20. Many evils have less to do with bad intentions or externalized harms and more to do with overefficiency and the tyranny of scale. A central and inevitable problem of the digital era is how to cultivate strong locality when we have such alluring access to the placeless-timeless. See also: Sherry Turkle on the challenges of conversation in the age of instant telecommunication, and Joshua Meyrowitz on the recent erosion of social life.

  21. We have really know idea what it might mean for software to be “malleable” and it’s quite embarrassing when we try to talk about it. This is evidence of the unseriousness of software design as a field. The next big hurdle for software design is to become a discipline; to do so require software design to articulate a theory of itself. For this it suffices to pose a set of meta-questions.

  22. Software designers, theorists, and critics can probably take a great deal of inspiration from the work of architectural phenomenologists and anthropologists. Those who study how a physical space feels and how people experience it and use it have likely developed methods that are also applicable to digital spaces. See Judith Donath.

  23. Phenomena and thick description, as compared to analysis and statistics, will only become more compelling. The most rarefied kind of insight is perspective. The most sought-after treasure is experience. It is a valuable and nontrivial skill to convey, deeply and expressively, what it is like to be something or to do something. —And it is also on the reader to carry out the interpretive work of appreciating perspective and partiality. The dogmatic mythos of rigor of computation and statistics in social studies will be relaxed and attention will swing towards ethnography, intuition, close reading, and “deep hanging out.”

  24. We all use the internet. Those who use it actively, as a medium for creative expression and interactive conversation, are significantly happier than those who use it passively as consumers of information and analgesics.

  25. The primary reason films and books and digital environments feel noticeably less tactile than they did in the past is not a change of medium or technology but a cultural shift in which people became disinterested in looking. The emphasis now is to know, and as a result we are impoverished. To look (and to notice, to sit, to observe, and to meditate) is an underratedly creative activity. As with any cultural trend it has a feedback mechanism: when filmmakers lose interest in looking at stuff, cinema does not teach young people how to look, or even to be interested in looking, and the next generation of artists makes things to be looked at without themselves ever being interested in looking at anything, and so what they make is not worth being looked at. Compare Dune, a totally unappealing film lauded for its cinematography and supposed tactility, with Cléo de 5 à 7, a film truly worth looking at.

  26. Video games are art. They must be critiqued as their own form of art: a medium centered on interactivity, dynamism, and simulation. Not as films or novels. The great potential of video games is their unique ability to confer perspective and experience through what is essentially software-mediated meditation. The trivial example is the Tetris effect.

  27. Fairy tales are very urgent and all around us.

  28. Reification is a useful move in design, communication, and art; essentially it is the action of the honest heart. It is dual with conceptualization/generalization/abstraction.

  29. We ask the question concerning software when we ask what it is.
