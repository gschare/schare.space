Computer science

The technical jargon of computer science turns out to be very handy in expediently articulating complex concepts to those with the right shared context. I‚Äôll give you an example of what I mean. While I was visiting MIT to decide if I wanted to do my PhD studies there, Adam Chlipala asked me, ‚Äúwhat state transition are you hoping to achieve by executing the PhD function?‚Äù Granted, this example can be expressed pretty well in normal-people-language, but that‚Äôs why it is good for illustration here: the non-computer scientists reading this may be able to infer what he was asking, which was simply, ‚Äúwhat are you hoping to achieve by doing a PhD?‚Äù or more specifically, ‚Äúhow do you expect to be different at the end of your PhD compared to now, and why is doing a PhD the best way to accomplish this change?‚Äù

I tend to draw my metaphors from basic data structures and algorithms, and from the field of programming languages and compilers. I also take some from pure mathematics.

My favorite is hill climbing. Coming from mathematical optimization, hill climbing studies the problem of finding an appropriate path to the highest point on a curved surface which may contain hills and valleys. There is one globally highest point, but if you only look greedily for ascending slopes, you may reach a lower local maximum. In the metaphor, this local maximum feels like the best you can hope for, because all other immediate paths seem to point downwards to lower levels of [happiness, wealth, comfort]. Metaphorically speaking, most complex problems we face in real life are not convex (there is not one obvious path to the best solution). Reaching the global maximum requires taking some risks and even accepting a temporarily worse situation before it gets better. One common example: therapy for mental health.

I have the kindling of an argument for why this pidgin can be so effective. Computer science is rich in techniques for talking about abstract things: defining them, comparing them, transforming them. Programmers tend to keep a question in the back of their minds at all times: ‚Äúhow can I make this more general?‚Äù Some people argue computer science is the study of abstraction. Others argue it is the study of complexity (and how to manage it effectively). So the century of work that has gone into developing the vocabulary, logics, and systems used by computer science for managing complexity can be pretty well applied to everyday scenarios that touch on this universal concept of complexity. Sometimes I think if you strip away the engineering aspects of computer science, what remains looks pretty much like applied philosophy.

Also, computer science, like any field, creates jargon like this in order to enable people to recognize common patterns so they can reuse old solutions to solved problems. There can be a ‚Äúwhen all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail‚Äù effect here, but when done well it is a great thing to be able to look at an obstacle and immediately reframe it in your mind into something you already know how to deal with. Indeed, in math and computer science, this technique has a formal term: reduction. Computer science theory (specifically complexity theory and computability theory) are more or less about learning one or two known solutions and then a whole host of techniques for reshaping new problems into a form that is compatible with those known solutions. Likewise, parsimony is highly valued in programming languages; the fewer basic abstractions you need and the more you can express one idea in terms of another, the more efficient your algorithm can be. That training, I think, generalizes to some extent so that the mind of a computer scientist is uniquely adept at memorizing general, abstract patterns and recalling them when equivalent configurations arise in the wild.

Tools for Conviviality by Ivan Illich

The (extremely brief) pitch of this book is: in order to achieve true progress and positive social outcomes from advances in technology, tools typically reserved to experts must become accessible to non-professionals. Examples include literacy, basic medical knowledge, and, for those computer scientists who read this book today, the art of programming.

Scientific empiricism

Oh man, I feel almost unqualified to write this here because I have not deeply studied Hume and Quine. Yes, I studied science in high school like everyone else, and yes, I considered myself an atheist or agnostic for a long time, and yes, I felt a kinship with those liberal democrat lawn signs that say ‚ÄúSCIENCE IS REAL!‚Äù But since learning some introductory philosophy of science, reading Tools for Conviviality, and getting exposed to other epistemological systems, I am a bit less obsessed with this. Still, a confidence in the practical predictive power of scientific empricism is central to my personal epistemology. I do not go around under the assumption that a demon from a thought experiment is deluding my senses, confirming my inductive hypotheses, or moving high-energy particles to low-energy areas and not the other way around. I tend, rather, to operate on the assumption that science is a pretty good approximation of what we might colloquially call ‚Äúobjective base reality.‚Äù Whether or not there actually exists an objective base reality is, unfortunately, a subject for a different area of philosophy.

For a related example, I did some work during undergrad for a lab called the Making and Knowing Project, which studies a very fascinating period of history in which European artisans began to do their work in ways that look like the beginnings of the scientific method. They were doing alchemy, essentially, but they were experimenting and writing down results and sharing knowledge amongst themselves. Not much later, Isaac Newton was forming mathematical theories of optics and gravity in the academy. The interplay of aristocratic and bourgeois proto-science shows us that the history of ideas has a lot to tell us about how we currently think about the world and the process by which we collectively develop a better understanding of it.

Game of Thrones

Though I remember the summer of 2023 as a time of joyful friendship, I spent a good deal of time alone with Game of Thrones and it is through this work that I came to appreciate the notion of an ‚Äúopen story‚Äù‚Äîthat is, a story set in a fictional world which feels expansive because the creator put immense effort into the details that convey verisimilitude. When done well, the result is a work that takes on a life of its own, permitting endless speculation and fanfiction. Game of Thrones is a work that achieves this. Every political maneuver is entirely consistent with the rest of the events occurring in the vast story, and consistent with the psychology of each character. It is mystifying how GRRM achieved this, and D&D are worthy of further praise for faithfully adapting it to the screen‚Ä¶ aside from the well-known grave error towards the end. I recall one afternoon I walked around running errands while staying on the phone with Rana the entire time, the two of us verbally creating an outline of how we would rewrite Season 8 to better conclude the story. In a sense, the total flop of the final season makes it all the more tantalizing to consider how it could have ended. Imperfection begets engagement, because we strive for perfection. Perhaps it was intentional.
