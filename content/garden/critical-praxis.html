  1. Life imitates art: in novels we read about characters who possessed interiority, so we invented individuality, as a technology, in order to be like them. This happened around the turn of the century, and now it is original sin. Inventions are supposed to solve problems; what problem did this one solve?

  2. Human-computer inter/action is the study of tools for pro/creativity and re/productivity.

  3. A man from Italy, just a bachelor, heard legend that there was a remarkable proverb on top of a mountain overlooking a small town in France. He set off with a party of servants and from a distance gazed upon the summit with trepidation. It was barren and stony. Despite the daylight sun he and his companions trailed persistently up and at the top he found nothing, no structure, no wise man, no paper and so certainly no proverb. The Scottish witches who sent him on this quest advertised that he would find wisdom and the key to all knowledges. He secretly pictured, with pleasure, a small paper note that would fit in his pocket; on the carriage to the town he thought often of how he would finger the proverb in his pocket, though he would have it memorized, and he dreamed of the beauty and perfection that he would attain in his life with the support of the proverb, and he felt ecstatic. On the mountain, the vision lurched up and he found himself at the foot of the misting cauldron, still with the witches. He thanked them and set off to locate the proverb.

    In France, the mountain was not as he saw in the cauldron. It was tree-topped and impossibly steep. The party paused at an inn of the small town. There he met a local scholar, a few years his senior, who dedicated his life to the study of esoteric knowledges. The man accompanied to the scholar to his tower in a small castle adjacent to the town, and saw his collection of books that filled every shelf along the rounded wall on two levels of the tower. The man understood at once the witches’ metaphor: there was deep wisdom to be found in the mountain of paper in the scholar’s collection. He asked the scholar to take him on as an apprentice, to aid in the study of the texts. The scholar’s reply to the man was noted in a letter he sent to his family, but we have lost the letter and no copies were made, so we cannot know the exact words he said. We understand from secondary sources about the letter that the scholar’s answer influenced the man to return at once to Italy, where he spent the rest of his life teaching at the university. The written versions of his lectures, recorded by his students, served as mandatory introductory curricula in the liberal arts at the university for several centuries. In all his writing after his encounter with the scholar, except for a single surviving letter which details these events themselves, there is no mention of a proverb.

  4. The quest for the Self is less like a journey or a search and more like a vulgar experiment: measurement necessarily disturbs. In the impure process of Self-creation we are observer-subjects.

  5. On the one hand, we can strain to make ourselves legible as archetypes. On the other hand, human beings do not fit neatly into archetypal categories; the Person (whatever kind of thing that is) bleeds through, immanently, irreducibly. Instead we can strain for years to make ourselves legible through Uncompromising Personhood.

  6. “People never change,” was said by someone who, being fictional, never changes, and who shares my name, which I, being real, changed.

  7. In metaphysics, bundle theory says that “identity” can be reduced to a bundle of properties; if two things share all properties—color, weight, age, etc.—then they are the same. Suppose a universe in which there exist only two iron spheres, indistinguishable in all qualities. Are they identical? No, because there is a final property that distinguishes them: their relationship to each other. That is to say, it is our relationships that make our identities—the prepositional aspects: who we are to each other, in each other, with each other.

  8. Boy Substack: The Latin word haecceitas (“thisness”) is Medieval in origin, coined by a Scottish Franciscan friar in the late 13th century in his magnum opus of theological commentary. Around the same time and several hundred miles to the south, the Welsh authors of chivalric tales codified the Arthurian legends: stories that featured not just knights and monsters and ladies in distress, but also wizards who could transmute lead into gold. Meanwhile, in England, early proto-scientists in the original universities penned the first modern treatises on chemical processes, based on crude experiments in rudimentary laboratories hardly distinguishable from village workshops. Writing on brittle, expensive, low-quality paper and lacking reliable means of communicating over long distances, these pious clergymen, these balladists of folklore, and these cloistered scholars all independently, and in their respective manners, posed and grappled with the philosophical question that later formed the basis of modern metaphysical inquiry: what determines the unique identity of a thing? In other words, the poets, chemists and holymen all sought after an understanding of essence.

    Girl Substack: I suck on a Ricola alone in the bedroom. There are so many rooms. I smell dead lavender, fresh air from the open window, ginseng, and sex. A baby cries outside; I hear it up to the sixth floor, echoing off the exposed brick. I slip into a bathrobe I find hanging on a bare metal hook and pull aside the curtain. Quiet, quiet, coos the mother. Instantly I feel jealous of this doting attention, this care—I miss the comforts of my own apartment, rosehip oil and those bright yellow sheets, and I miss taking sick days off school as a child back in California, spending the whole afternoon alone on the couch watching Victorious. I’ve spent eight years in this city and still without a novel to show for it. The morning edges towards midday. Nowhere to be. My gaze drifts to the Smith-Corona on the dark wooden desk in the middle of the room. Why not now? Joan Didion would not hesitate. Still in a dream I sit in the rigid chair, roll a page into the platen, and consciousness erupts onto paper. The air conditioner lodged in the window rumbles and starts.

  9. There is a great wave building, if you can feel it. The moment of its crash will have the quality of triumphant evolution, the ape brandishing the tool designed, this time, for creation and, thrown aloft, this mallet marks the long-awaited discovery of how to communicate with each other, affirming the technological species entitlement to the ontological crafts—the world-historical redemption of modernism.

  10. Often it is the case that we are without words to express our feelings. And very often words themselves lead us astray, in their dangerous imprecision. The way through misunderstanding and speechlessness is art. It is an art to express anything of real complexity and real importance. “The writer says in words what cannot be said in words.”

  11. Artists hitherto have only Romanticized the world; the point is to Modernize it.
