“Project proposal” proposal: artist housing affordability

Dear beloved project partners Andre, Gabrielle, and Kendall,

I’d like to elaborate on the proposal I mentioned to each of you at the beginning of class a month ago.

The six themes suggested for the final project are so cool, so great. But we are strong, independent researchers and I would like us to take heed of Arvind’s note:

Your group can also choose to work on something not listed here.

I propose we center on our final visualization project on artist housing in Greater Boston. This relates directly to housing affordability—the “starving artist” stereotype has its basis in reality—and connects the moral and economic good of affordable housing to the cultural good of a society that has strong support for young artists to focus on their work, and the challenges therein.

I will spend the rest of this letter detailing my movation, some aspects of the theme, and potential data sources.

The morphine angel had said sometimes you could get a room at the Chelsea Hotel in exchange for art. It was my intention to offer our work. I believed the drawings I had done in Paris were strong and there was no question Robert’s work eclipsed anything hanging in the lobby. My first hurdle would be Stanley Bard, the hotel manager. . . .

I got the go-ahead to reenter Mr. Bard’s office. I took the positive approach. I told him I was on my way to get an advance from my employer but would give him the opportunity to have work that was worth far more than rent. I sang Robert’s praises, offering our portfolios as collateral. Bard was skeptical but he gave me the benefit of the doubt. I don’t know if the prospect of seeing our work meant anything to him but he seemed impressed with my pledge of employment. We shook hands and I palmed the key. Room 1017. Fifty-five dollars a week to live in the Chelsea Hotel. . . . We were home.

Patti Smith, Just Kids

In Patti Smith’s memoir she details her risky move from midwestern farm country to New York City in the late 60s, spending several days homeless in Central Park with other bohemians before finding a safe place to sleep, and several years scrounging pennies to pay for sandwiches (starving artist indeed). Long before she became the leading pioneer of punk rock (as well as a widely-respected writer, poet, and photographer), she scraped by an existence in the grimy artist underground of New York through little more than luck and the goodwill of benevolent landlords like Mr. Bard.

This is probably not enough, and as we explore the topic I am sure we will think of new questions and search for new data with which to investigate it. I’m excited about the possibility of translating into rigorous visualizations some of the intuitions—could even call them myths or apocrypha—I hold about artist housing, like the spatial politics and cultural vibrance of the Harlem Renaissance, the Beatniks’ 1940-50s invasion of the cheap post-industrial Greenwich Village (now severely gentrified), Basquiat’s 1980s Times Square hip-hop counterculture 3, Hemingway and Stein’s alcoholic expatriotic “Moveable Feast” in 1920s Paris, the Rust Belt’s pop art intellectual export, and, to contrast these scrappy kids with an artist scene based in opulent wealth and fame, the 70s Hollywood film and music industry of Didion, Babitz, Morrison. Of course, while the wider history is interesting, we have to focus our effort and attention on Boston. The questions in my mind that guide this investigation include:

  • Have the number of artists in Boston changed over time? What trends do they correlate with?
  • What has been or will be the impact of artist-focused affordable housing policies?
  • How do arts districts (both planned and emergent) interact with systems of gentrification and racial discrimination?
  • Where do art students in Greater Boston live before and after graduation and what do they do for work? Has this changed over time? Does it correlate with any particular social, economic, or housing trends?
  • Can we correlate the above trends with data from culture/entertainment industries, including but not limited to: museum visitorship, music sales, arts spending…

I want to understand, most essentially, how the housing crisis is affecting our aesthetic culture, by literally inhibiting the formation of the kind of busy urban artist circles that were responsible for basically every major artistic scene in at least 20th century US history, if not universally, forever. Boston is potentially a very interesting case because of its long history as a major city (relative to others) and its large student population.

This will be a greater challenge than the official themes because it will be harder to obtain data—because we will have to find it ourselves, and because there isn’t much data on this topic to begin with—and we will be blazing our own path of research. But it will be a lot more fun (and I will love you forever).
